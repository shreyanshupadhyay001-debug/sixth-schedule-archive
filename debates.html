Overview of the Debates

The Sixth Schedule was debated in the Constituent Assembly primarily over three days — 5th, 6th, and 7th September 1949 — as part of Volume IX of the official record. The debates were preceded by consideration of the Fifth Schedule (Scheduled Areas and Scheduled Tribes) on 5th September, and the discussion of the two instruments was closely interconnected.

The key architect of the Sixth Schedule was Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, who moved Amendment No. 98 (adding the words "Subject to the provisions of this paragraph" before Para 1) and Amendment No. 99 (substituting a new sub-paragraph (3) for Para 1). The most significant revisions concerned the Governor's power to define boundaries, create new autonomous districts, and merge existing ones — changes designed to make the Schedule operationally flexible.

State-wise Debate Analysis

Explore how the debates addressed the specific concerns of each state that would eventually fall under the Sixth Schedule.

Assam Most Debated State Original Schedule State

Assam was the primary territory for which the Sixth Schedule was conceived. The 1949 debates contain extensive discussion of the Assam Hills Districts — the Khasi and Jaintia Hills, the Garo Hills, the Lushai Hills (now Mizoram), the Naga Hills, the North Cachar Hills, and the Mikir Hills. The key debates involving Assam centred on the powers of the Governor, the composition of the Commission under Para 14, and the autonomy of the Hill tribes.

KMC
Shri Kuladhar Chaliha
Assam: General · 5th September 1949 · Pages 1004–1006
▼

Shri Chaliha raised the most substantive objection on behalf of the Assam members — questioning the composition and necessity of the Commission under the proviso to Para 1(3). He moved an amendment for deletion of the proviso:

"I want both the provisos to be taken out but here one proviso has been taken out but the other remains in the next paragraph. If you look at para 14 you will find this: 'provision of educational and medical facilities and communications in such districts; the need for any new or special legislation in respect of such districts; and the administration of the laws, regulations and rules made by the District and Regional Councils.' But you do not find mention of these subjects in paragraph 3. It mentions something else."

Chaliha's core concern was the potential for delay created by mandatory Commission consultation before boundary changes — particularly in the context of Assam's unstable political situation with pressures from Pakistan, China, and Burma. He preferred direct Governor action guided by Cabinet advice.

"A Governor means of course the Cabinet. I do not want a Commission. The Governor would have the power in consultation with his Cabinet to discuss these things and if it is left to a Commission there will be obvious delay."

Amendment moved ▼ Negatived
BP
Shri Brajeshwar Prasad
Bihar: General · 5th September 1949 · Pages 1005–1008
▼

Brajeshwar Prasad proposed the most radical alternative — that the entire tribal areas of Assam be placed under Central administration. He moved three amendments including substituting "President" for "Governor" throughout, and adding a clause making the Governor an agent of the President.

"I am opposed to handing over the administration of the tribal areas into the hands of the provincial government, because Assam is on the border of five or six foreign States. I am referring to China, Tibet, Burma and Pakistan. Sir, in Assam, the conflicts between the Ahoms, and the Assamese, the Bengalees and the Muslims and the Mangoloid races have assumed proportions of which probably we the members of the House are not fully aware and so do not realise the gravity of the situation with which the Government of Assam is confronted."

Ambedkar responded that while frontier areas were addressed separately under the Schedule (with the Governor acting under the President in those areas), wholesale centralisation would create an impossible constitutional anomaly of a province with parts administered by the Centre.

3 Amendments moved ▼ All Negatived
BRA
Dr. B.R. Ambedkar — Reply on Assam Amendments
Bombay: General · 5th September 1949 · Pages 1006–1008
▼

"Sir, there are just two points which have been raised in the course of the remarks made on these amendments which call for reply. The first question is the one, which was raised by Mr. Chaliha. I must say I was somewhat surprised at the amendment tabled by Mr. Chaliha, because like the Fifth Schedule the Sixth Schedule also has arisen, so to say, out of an agreement between the Drafting Committee and the Premier of Assam, my Friend Mr. Nichols Roy and at which conference Mr. Chaliha also was present, and he accepted the new schedule as amended by the Drafting Committee."

Ambedkar further explained that the Commission mechanism in Para 14 was not an obstacle to effective governance — the Commission's composition and terms of reference would be guided by the Cabinet, since the Governor acts on Cabinet advice (not in discretion) in this context.

"It is quite obvious that in constituting the Commission, and defining its terms of reference, the Governor would be guided by the advice of the local ministers, and I do not think, therefore, there need be any fears such as the one that he has expressed."

"He has forgotten to take note of the fact that even in constituting the autonomous areas, the Drafting Committee has not forgotten that there are what are called certain 'frontier areas', bordering on the autonomous districts. It has been provided in this Schedule that so far as the administration of these frontier areas of Assam is concerned, the Governor would be acting under the President. Consequently whatever strategic importance, the frontier areas may have, the Centre would certainly have ample jurisdiction to see that none of the disturbing factors to which he has made reference will find any place there."

TK
Shri T.T. Krishnamachari
Madras: General · 5th September 1949 · Page 1004
▼

Krishnamachari intervened briefly to draw the Assembly's attention to Amendment No. 134 tabled by the Drafting Committee, which addressed Chaliha's concern about the scope of the Commission's inquiry under Para 14.

"If he reads Amendment 134 tabled by the Drafting Committee, he will find the answer to his query. It covers these."

Meghalaya Post-1972 Statehood

In 1949, the territories that now constitute Meghalaya — the Khasi Hills, Jaintia Hills, and Garo Hills — were autonomous districts within Assam. The constituent debates addressed these districts as integral to the Assam framework. Meghalaya gained statehood in 1972, and the Sixth Schedule continued to apply to its three hill districts.

JS
Shri Jaipal Singh
Bihar: General (representing tribal interests broadly) · Pages 977–978, 992–996
▼

Jaipal Singh, the only Adivasi member of the Constituent Assembly, was a vocal and often contentious voice in the tribal debates. He moved five amendments to the Fifth Schedule (including extending reports on scheduled tribes, strengthening the Advisory Council, and confining benefits to tribes beyond scheduled areas) — all of which were ultimately negatived or withdrawn. His most significant contribution to the Sixth Schedule debate was his insistence that tribal governance must be effective, not merely formal.

"I find that this new proposed Fifth Schedule has, somehow or other, perhaps without meaning it, emasculated the Tribes Advisory Council. The whole pattern of the original draft was to bring the Tribes Advisory Council into action. It could initiate, originate things, but, somehow or other, the tables have now been turned. The initiative is placed in the hands of the Governor or Ruler of the State. I regret that that is a situation I cannot accept."

On the specific question of the Khasi Hills (which would become part of Meghalaya), Jaipal Singh consistently argued that tribal interests required elected, empowered councils — not merely advisory bodies.

Tripura Part C State → Union Territory → State

Tripura was a Part C State (formerly a princely state) at the time of the 1949 debates. Its tribal areas were governed under separate instruments during the transition period. The Sixth Schedule was extended to Tripura's tribal areas (now the Tripura Tribal Areas Autonomous District Council) following its merger with India and subsequent reorganisation as a full state in 1972.

DB
Shri Biswanath Das
Orissa: General · Pages 996–999 (general tribal debate)
▼

Biswanath Das offered the most philosophically challenging critique of the tribal schedule framework from a member who claimed shared identity with tribal peoples. He warned that scheduled areas risked perpetuating the colonial "museum" logic of keeping tribes separate from mainstream society.

"Though it has been thought wisdom for over a hundred years or more by British Imperialists to keep these tribal people and these Scheduled areas as museums for purposes of demonstration and exhibition before the world to justify their existence in India, what is the purpose today — to perpetuate this evil? There is absolutely no purpose. We are committed to a programme of social regeneration."

Despite his critique, Das ultimately recognised the practical necessity of transitional protections, and expressed particular concern about the powers vested in Rajpramukhs — hereditary rulers who he feared could exploit tribal sentiment for political purposes.

Mizoram Lushai Hills District (1949)

In 1949, the Lushai Hills (now Mizoram) were a district of Assam and among the areas most clearly contemplated for autonomous district status under the Sixth Schedule. The region had a distinctive history of exclusion under the colonial regime. Mizoram became a Union Territory in 1972 and a full state in 1987, inheriting the Sixth Schedule framework for its districts.

AVT
Shri A.V. Thakkar (Thakkar Bapa)
Saurashtra · Pages 989–991 (on Lushai Hills specifically)
▼

Thakkar Bapa, widely respected as the leading non-tribal advocate for tribal welfare in the Assembly, provided the most detailed empirical account of conditions in the various tribal areas. He specifically highlighted the Lushai Hills as one of the most isolated and least-known tribal regions.

"Not only that, but the Tribal Advisory Committee will consist of three-fourth of tribal members. They can if they like, take the greatest advantage of it in all ameliorative measures as well as in the conduct of everyday affairs of the Scheduled tribes, as well as Scheduled areas, but I am afraid our tribal friends are too shy yet. They have to be brought out not only from the plains of the country but also from the hills and hilltops, from the distant Himachal, from the distant Vindhyachal, from the Hills of Chota Nagpur, from the hills of Travancore and Cochin."

"I will give you only one instance. When I went with the Assam Tribal Committee to tour in the areas of Assam with the Chairman Mr. Gopinath Bardolai and the prominent Minister Rev. Nichols Roy all the members of the Committee, one and all, went for the first time to the Lushai Hills and Naga Hills in the year 1947. Even the Premier of Assam had never visited the Lushai Hills and Naga Hills, much less a man like me."

Nagaland Naga Hills District (1949) Statehood 1963

The Naga Hills were, in 1949, among the most politically sensitive areas discussed in the Constituent Assembly. The Naga National Council had already articulated demands for sovereignty. The Sixth Schedule framework was partly designed to provide a constitutional accommodation — giving Naga Hills an autonomous district with wide self-governance powers without conceding the right of secession. Nagaland became India's 16th state in 1963 under a separate constitutional arrangement (Article 371A).

KM
Shri K.M. Munshi
Bombay: General · Pages 999–1001
▼

Munshi offered the most comprehensive defence of the Drafting Committee's approach, directly addressing Jaipal Singh's amendments and explaining the philosophy of managed integration — protecting tribal communities while preventing their permanent separateness.

"We want that the Scheduled tribes in the whole country should be protected from the destructive compact of races possessing a higher and more aggressive culture and should be encouraged to develop their own autonomous life; at the same time we want them to take a larger part in the life of the country adopted. They should not be isolated communities or little republics to be perpetuated for ever."

On the Naga Hills specifically, Munshi argued that the Sixth Schedule's autonomous district model — with its wide legislative, judicial, and administrative powers — provided the best available constitutional accommodation for peoples with distinct governance traditions, short of outright sovereignty.

5 September 1949 — Sixth Schedule Opens

After disposing of the Fifth Schedule in the morning session, the Assembly turned to the Sixth Schedule. President Rajendra Prasad took the chair. Dr. Ambedkar moved two amendments on behalf of the Drafting Committee.

Dr. Ambedkar — Moving Amendment No. 98 (Para 1, Sub-para 1)
▼

Text of Amendment No. 98:

"That in sub-paragraph (1) of paragraph 1, before the words, 'The tribal areas' the words 'Subject to the provisions of this paragraph' be inserted."

Ambedkar's Explanation: "Originally, the draft merely said that the Tribal areas were those which were included in the table attached to this Schedule. There was no power given to define the boundaries of those areas included in the Table. It is felt that it is necessary to give the Governor the power to define the boundaries of those areas included in the Table. In order to provide for this power for the Governor, it is necessary to add the words which are contained in this amendment."

✓ Adopted
Dr. Ambedkar — Moving Amendment No. 99 (Para 1, Sub-para 3)
▼

Text of Amendment No. 99 — New Sub-paragraph (3):

"(3) The Governor may, by public notification—
(a) include any area in Part I of the said Table,
(b) create a new autonomous district,
(c) increase the area of any autonomous district,
(d) diminish the area of any autonomous district,
(e) unite two or more autonomous districts or parts thereof so as to form one autonomous district,
(f) define the boundaries of any autonomous district:

Provided that no order shall be made by the Governor under clauses (b), (c), (d) and (e) of this sub-paragraph except after consideration of the report of a Commission appointed under sub-paragraph (1) of paragraph 14 of this Schedule."

Ambedkar's Explanation: "In this amendment, the new things to which attention must be drawn are, included in sub-clauses (e) and (f) of sub-paragraph (3). That is necessary because it may be required, in any particular state of affairs, that two or more autonomous districts may be united together. The power contained in sub-clause (f) is also necessary because it may be desirable to define the boundaries in case there is any particular dispute between the different tribes."

"It is better to make it uniform by requiring the Governor to act after consideration of the report of a Commission which is proposed to be appointed under sub-paragraph (1) of paragraph 14 of this Schedule."

✓ Adopted
Chaliha & Brajeshwar Prasad — Challenging Amendments (Negatived)
▼

Three amendments were moved against the Drafting Committee's proposals:

Chaliha — Delete the Proviso to Sub-para (3)
▼

Moved deletion of the entire proviso requiring a Commission report before creating, increasing, diminishing, or merging autonomous districts. Argued that Commission involvement would cause delay and that the Governor acting on Cabinet advice was sufficient safeguard.

▼ Negatived
Brajeshwar Prasad — Substitute "President" for "Governor"
▼

Moved that in sub-paras (2) and (3) of Para 1, "Governor" be replaced with "President" — effectively bringing Assam's tribal areas under direct central control. Also moved that the Governor should function as "agent of the President" in exercising Para 1 functions.

▼ All three amendments negatived
